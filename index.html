Liveable Neighbourhoods Explained

Liveable Neighbourhoods are residential neighbourhoods with traffic filters inside them to stop cars, vans and lorries using the streets as rat-runs.

Liveable Neighbourhoods are a huge boon to local businesses. High streets in these areas become attractive destinations where people want to spend their time again and again, and so footfall and trade increase.

There’s now a groundswell of public support for low-traffic neighbourhoods, but the council won’t provide them if we don’t show we want them.

Liveable Neighbourhoods FAQ

For too long streets have been treated simply as corridors that facilitate the movement of traffic. But they are so much more than that. Our streets also serve an important ‘place’ function where we meet and socialise, where our children play, where our local businesses are, where we walk and cycle, and where the public life of our neighbourhoods plays out. There are ways we can improve the place function of our streets and make them friendly for people and not just cars, while simultaneously improving our air quality and improving our health. However this doesn’t need to be to the detriment of car drivers so it really can be a win-win for all parties.

The organisation Living Streets define Liveable Neighbourhoods (also known as Low Traffic Neighbourhoods, Access Only Neighbourhoods or Mini-Hollands) as follows:

Low traffic neighbourhoods are groups of residential streets, bordered by main or 'distributor’ roads (the places where buses, lorries, non-local traffic should be), where 'through' motor vehicle traffic is discouraged or removed. There’s lots of ways you can make a low traffic neighbourhood, but the main principle is that every resident can drive onto their street, get deliveries etc., but it’s harder or impossible to drive straight through from one main road to the next. With through traffic gone, the streets in a low traffic neighbourhood see dramatic reductions in motor traffic levels and often speeds too.

One major aspect of Liveable Neighbourhoods is the removal of rat-runs to ensure that the only cars using residential streets are to access the residential area itself, with through traffic instead using more appropriate perimeter routes.

Liveable Neighbourhoods involve using 'connectors' or ‘modal filters’ that allow pedestrians and cyclists to pass through but not motor traffic. These connectors are placed at strategic locations around the neighbourhood and can be made using bollards or planters.

These connectors prevent neighbourhoods being used as rat runs for non-local through traffic, while retaining full access for local residents and visitors. The connectors are located so that no attractive through routes remain across the neighbourhood, to ensure that traffic isn't just shifted to other residential streets.

A number of London Boroughs have introduced Liveable Neighbourhoods, which provide a useful evidence base for their impacts. A Westminster University study on the impacts of Liveable Neighbourhoods which have been introduced in London identified that:

After one year, people living in parts of such boroughs were, on average, walking and cycling for 41 minutes a week more than those living in comparable areas.

These Liveable Neighbourhood schemes have also been attributed with an increase in life expectancy of between seven to nine months for local residents due to a combination of increased activity and improved air quality.

However, Liveable Neighbourhoods don’t just encourage walking and cycling and improve air quality. They can change the entire nature of residential areas including:

  • Children being able to play in the streets.
  • Local businesses able to provide more outdoor seating.
  • Tree planting and landscaping to make streets greener.
  • Public realm improvements to make an area more visually attractive.
  • Level surfaces to assist wheelchair and pushchair users.

In short, no. Due to 'traffic evaporation' around 15% of displaced traffic can disappear from the area entirely as drivers adjust routes and behaviour – either avoiding the area, changing modes or even cancelling journeys. A Westminster University study concluded that:

There was no evidence that time spent in cars was increasing (due to congestion).

We all know that the high street is struggling. Understandably business owners will be concerned about how any changes might affect their business and their livelihood. Local residents want to see thriving high streets and no vacant units. Fortunately, far from representing a threat to local businesses, Liveable Neighbourhoods are a great opportunity to increase footfall and increase trade. There is now a wealth of evidence which shows that pedestrian and cycle friendly schemes are a boon to high streets and are credited with average increases in footfall of 32% and retail turnover by 17% as a direct result of improvements to the pedestrian environment.

This is intuitive. With the rise of internet shopping high streets need to serve more than just a basic retail function and become destinations where people enjoy spending their time. If public spaces are inviting then people will return time after time and in doing so will support their local businesses, creating vibrant and thriving high streets.

The impact of pedestrian and cycle friendly schemes on local high streets is summarised by this excellent Sustrans article and the benefit of public realm improvements to local high streets is discussed at length in this Transport for London report.

Liveable Neighbourhoods have been implemented successfully in the UK, most notably in the London Borough of Waltham Forest with the schemes widely hailed as a huge success, and proving popular despite some initial opposition. A gallery of before and after photos from Waltham Forest demonstrate what could also be achieved in Bristol with enough popular and political support.

Bristol Cycling Campaign recently hosted a webinar on Liveable Neighbourhoods, including a presentation from expert Dan Kelly, which provided a fascinating discussion and a tour of some of the Waltham Forest schemes. This is well worth a watch.

The Draft Local Walking and Cycling Infrastructure Plan for the West of England has already identified St Werburgh’s and Southville as two areas of Bristol where Liveable Neighbourhoods could be implemented and Councillors Kye Dudd and Nicola Beech have both toured Liveable Neighbourhood schemes in Waltham Forest. So it’s very likely that they will come to some areas of Bristol in the future.

The Draft Local Walking and Cycling Infrastructure Plan covers the period 2020-2036 so that equates to two Liveable Neighbourhoods over the next 15 years...

Liveable Neighbourhoods can be cheap to implement compared to other transport schemes, as often a trial can be conducted with as little as a number of strategically placed bollards, planters or outdoor seating. So is the current level of ambition really good enough? Should we really have to wait decades for cleaner air and safer streets when it’s possible now?

The Liveable Neighbourhoods for Bristol Campaign is about accelerating the Liveable Neighbourhoods programme and getting Liveable Neighbourhoods implemented in all areas of the city in the shortest possible time.

Every neighbourhood in Bristol deserves clean air and safe streets so Liveable Neighbourhoods should be implemented across the whole of the city, not just in the two locations already identified.

Liveable Neighbourhoods can initially be trialled for 12-18 months before they are made permanent if they are popular. This way local residents and businesses are in control of the process and if the scheme is unpopular then it can be altered or removed entirely.

Liveable Neighbourhoods won’t happen without popular support from local people in their local area. So please sign the petition then use the tool above to find a Liveable Neighbourhoods group in your area and start convincing your local Councillors and Mayor that the time for change has come!
